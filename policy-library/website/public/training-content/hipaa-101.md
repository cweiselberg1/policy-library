## What is HIPAA?

### Definition and Purpose

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) is a federal law enacted in 1996 that protects the privacy and security of health information. HIPAA establishes national standards for protecting patient privacy while allowing appropriate use and disclosure of health information for treatment, payment, and healthcare operations.

### Key Legislation

- **Privacy Rule** (2003) - Controls how health information is used and disclosed
- **Security Rule** (2005) - Sets standards for protecting electronic protected health information (ePHI)
- **Breach Notification Rule** (2009) - Requires notification when patient data is compromised
- **HITECH Act** (2009) - Enhanced penalties and extended HIPAA applicability

### Who Must Comply?

**Covered Entities:**
- Healthcare providers (hospitals, physicians, clinics, mental health providers)
- Health plans (health insurance companies, HMOs, employer health plans)
- Healthcare clearinghouses

**Business Associates:**
- IT vendors and software companies
- Billing and collection agencies
- Data analysis companies
- Transcription services
- Medical record storage services
- Any third party handling PHI on your behalf

### Why HIPAA Matters

Patient privacy is a fundamental right. HIPAA ensures:
- Patients know how their information is used
- Patients can access their own medical records
- Healthcare organizations maintain confidentiality
- Data breaches are detected and reported
- Trust is maintained in the healthcare system

## The Privacy Rule

### What is the Privacy Rule?

The Privacy Rule sets the national standards for the use and disclosure of Protected Health Information (PHI). It applies to all patient health records in any formatâ€”paper, electronic, oral, or other media.

### Core Principles

**Minimum Necessary Standard**
- Disclose only the minimum amount of information needed to accomplish the intended purpose
- Limit access to PHI to employees who need it to perform their job functions
- Example: A billing clerk does not need access to psychiatric history

**Standard Authorization**
- Written patient consent must be obtained before using or disclosing health information for most purposes
- Patients have the right to read, request corrections to, and obtain copies of their records
- Separate consent required for specific uses (research, marketing, psychotherapy notes)

**Permitted Uses and Disclosures**

*Without Patient Authorization:*
- Treatment and patient care
- Payment and billing operations
- Healthcare operations (quality improvement, staff training, credentialing)
- Public health activities (disease reporting, FDA surveillance)
- Law enforcement (with court orders or proper legal process)
- National security and defense
- Judicial and administrative proceedings

*Requires Patient Authorization:*
- Marketing (unless face-to-face or for healthcare operations)
- Research (except certain limited datasets)
- Psychotherapy notes (highly protected)
- Sale of health information

### De-identified Information

Information that has been stripped of 18 specific identifiers can be used and disclosed without restrictions:

1. Name
2. Address (all geographic subdivisions smaller than state)
3. Phone and fax numbers
4. Email addresses
5. Medical record numbers
6. Health insurance numbers
7. Account numbers
8. Certificate and license numbers
9. Vehicle identifiers and license plate numbers
10. Device identifiers and serial numbers
11. Web URLs
12. Internet Protocol (IP) addresses
13. Biometric identifiers
14. Full-face photographs and comparable images
15. Dates (except year) related to the individual
16. Any other unique identifying number, characteristic, or code
17. Dates of admission/discharge
18. Zip codes (except for first three digits)

### Business Associate Agreements

If your organization shares PHI with outside vendors or contractors, a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) must be in place that:
- Specifies permitted uses and disclosures of PHI
- Requires the same privacy and security safeguards
- Includes liability terms and breach notification requirements
- Includes provisions for return or destruction of PHI upon request

## The Security Rule

### Overview

The Security Rule establishes national standards to protect electronic protected health information (ePHI) from unauthorized access, use, or disclosure. It focuses on information systems and how electronic data is handled, transmitted, and stored.

### Three Key Safeguards

#### 1. Administrative Safeguards
Controls and policies for managing security measures.

**Required Measures:**
- Security management process identifying risks and vulnerabilities
- Designated security officer responsible for compliance
- Information access management limiting access to ePHI
- Security awareness training for all employees
- Security incident procedures for reporting and responding to breaches
- Contingency planning for emergency access to ePHI

#### 2. Physical Safeguards
Controls protecting facilities and equipment from unauthorized access.

**Required Measures:**
- Access controls (badges, locks, surveillance)
- Workstation security policies
- Secure disposal of hardware containing ePHI
- Device and media controls (encryption, inventory tracking)
- Facility access controls and monitoring

#### 3. Technical Safeguards
Technology-based security measures for protecting ePHI.

**Required Measures:**
- Access controls (user IDs, passwords, multi-factor authentication)
- Encryption of data in transit and at rest
- Audit controls logging all access to ePHI
- Integrity controls preventing unauthorized modification
- Transmission security protecting data during electronic transmission
- Regular security testing and monitoring

### Encryption Requirements

All ePHI must be protected with:
- **Data at Rest** - Encrypted on servers, laptops, USB drives, and backup systems
- **Data in Transit** - Encrypted when transmitted over networks (HTTPS, VPN, secure file transfer)
- **Encryption Keys** - Securely managed and backed up
- **Two-Factor Authentication** - Required for remote access to systems containing ePHI

### Audit Logs and Monitoring

Organizations must:
- Maintain comprehensive logs of who accessed what information and when
- Review logs regularly for suspicious activity
- Track failed login attempts
- Monitor for unusual access patterns
- Retain audit logs for at least 6 years

## Protected Health Information (PHI)

### What Constitutes PHI?

Any health information that can be linked to a specific individual is considered PHI. This includes:

**Medical Information**
- Diagnoses, symptoms, and test results
- Medications and treatment plans
- Mental health and psychiatric records
- Substance abuse and HIV status
- Medical histories

**Personal Identifiers**
- Name, address, phone number
- Social Security number
- Medical record or health insurance numbers
- Email addresses
- Date of birth and age

**Payment Information**
- Billing records and insurance claims
- Payment history and outstanding balances
- Insurance authorization details

**Biometric Data**
- Fingerprints, retinal scans, genetic information
- Photographs and video recordings in clinical context

### Electronic Protected Health Information (ePHI)

ePHI is PHI in electronic format, including:
- Electronic health records (EHRs)
- Messages sent via email or secure messaging systems
- Data on computer servers and databases
- Data on USB drives, mobile devices, and laptops
- Fax cover sheets with PHI
- Screenshots containing patient information

**Critical Rule:** ePHI on mobile devices and removable media must be encrypted.

### PHI in Different Settings

**Hospital/Clinic Setting**
- Patient registration forms
- Medical charts and records
- Medication lists
- Lab results and imaging reports
- Nurse notes and progress notes

**Billing/Insurance Setting**
- Insurance claim forms
- Explanation of Benefits (EOB) documents
- Payment records
- Eligibility information

**Research Setting**
- Study participant lists
- De-identified research data (if properly stripped of identifiers)
- Consent forms and research protocols

## Patient Rights

### Access to Medical Records

Patients have the right to:
- Obtain a copy of their complete medical record
- Access information within 30 days of request
- Receive records in electronic format (if available)
- Request records be sent directly to another provider
- Request records be available via patient portal

**Your Responsibility:**
- Respond promptly to records requests
- Ensure complete and accurate copies
- Track all disclosure of records
- Maintain records of who accessed each file

### Right to Request Amendments

Patients can request corrections or additions to their medical records if they believe information is:
- Incomplete
- Inaccurate
- Untimely
- Irrelevant

**Organizations must:**
- Respond to amendment requests within 60 days
- Make approved amendments across all systems
- Document the amendment with date and authorship
- Notify all recipients of the original record

### Right to Request Restrictions

Patients may request restrictions on how their information is used or disclosed, such as:
- Limiting use to specific treatment providers
- Restricting marketing communications
- Requesting information not be shared with family members
- Limiting payment authorization disclosures

**Important:** Organizations can accept or decline restrictions, but must honor requests in writing.

### Right to Receive Confidential Communications

Patients can request to receive health information:
- At an alternative address or phone number
- Through alternative means (email, secure messaging)
- At a different time or location
- Without specifying a reason

### Right to an Accounting of Disclosures

Patients can request a list of who received their PHI, including:
- Name and organization of recipients
- Date and purpose of disclosure
- Information disclosed
- Exceptions: Treatment, payment, healthcare operations disclosures; law enforcement; national security

## Breach Notification Rule

### What Constitutes a Breach?

A breach is the **unauthorized acquisition, access, use, or disclosure** of PHI that compromises the security or privacy of the information.

### Four-Factor Risk Assessment

Not all unauthorized access is a reportable breach. A breach is only reportable if there is a significant risk of harm:

1. **Nature and Extent** - How much information was involved? What types?
2. **Unauthorized Persons** - Was it someone inside or outside the organization?
3. **Information Actually Acquired or Viewed** - Was the data actually read, or just accessed?
4. **Mitigation Measures** - Has the breached information been recovered or secured?

**Low Risk Scenarios (No Breach Report Required):**
- Password-protected file emailed to wrong internal recipient who immediately deletes it
- Encrypted data lost in transit (encryption key cannot be compromised)
- Data accessed but not viewed by unauthorized person
- Good faith belief no PHI was actually compromised

**High Risk Scenarios (Breach Report Required):**
- Unencrypted laptop containing patient records stolen from employee's car
- Email with PHI sent to wrong external recipient
- Unauthorized employee accessing and viewing patient records
- Ransomware attack exposing patient data
- USB drive with patient information lost in public

### Breach Notification Requirements

**Notify Within 60 Days:**
- Each affected individual (by mail, email, or phone)
- Clearly stating what information was breached
- What the organization is doing to prevent future breaches
- Steps individuals can take to protect themselves

**Media Notification:**
- If breach affects 500+ individuals, notify local media
- Notify state Attorney General
- Notify major newspapers and broadcast stations

**HHS Notification:**
- Notify Department of Health & Human Services
- Provide list of affected individuals and breach details

### Individual Notification Content

Must include:
- Date of breach and date discovered
- Description of what occurred
- Type of information involved
- Steps individuals should take
- What the organization is doing
- Contact information for questions
- Information about credit monitoring or identity theft protection services (if applicable)

## Violations and Penalties

### Civil Penalties

The Office for Civil Rights (OCR) enforces HIPAA compliance. Penalties are tiered based on violation category and knowledge level:

**Penalty Tiers (per violation, per day):**

| Category | Unknowing | Reasonable Cause | Willful Neglect | Willful Neglect (Not Corrected) |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------------------------|
| A | $100-$50,000 | $50,000-$100,000 | $50,000-$100,000 | $100,000+ |
| B | $1,000-$100,000 | $100,000-$200,000 | $100,000-$200,000 | $200,000+ |
| C | $10,000-$500,000 | $500,000-$1M | $500,000-$1M | $1M+ |
| D | $50,000-$1.5M | $1.5M-$5M | $1.5M-$5M | $5M+ |

**Real-World Examples:**
- University of Washington Medical Center: $750,000 (improper access to 411 patient records)
- Anthem Inc.: $115 million (unauthorized access to 78.8 million people's records)
- Advocate Medical Group: $5.55 million (inadequate administrative safeguards)
- Community Health Systems: $167 million (inadequate security measures leading to breach)

### Criminal Penalties

Criminal violations can result in:
- **Tier 1** (Knowingly obtaining or disclosing PHI): Up to $250,000 fine and 10 years imprisonment
- **Tier 2** (False pretense to obtain PHI): Up to $100,000 fine and 5 years imprisonment
- **Tier 3** (With intent to sell or transfer): Up to $250,000 fine and 10 years imprisonment

### Organizational Liability

- Entities liable for violations by employees and business associates
- Failure to implement required safeguards is strict liability (intent not required)
- Corrective action plans may be required
- Business reputation damage and patient trust loss

### Common Violations

**Privacy Rule Violations:**
- Disclosing PHI without authorization
- Failing to provide patient access to records
- Using or disclosing more information than necessary
- No written consent for research participation
- Improper marketing communications

**Security Rule Violations:**
- Unencrypted ePHI on mobile devices
- Inadequate access controls and authentication
- No audit logs or incomplete audit trails
- Failing to conduct security risk analysis
- Insufficient employee training
- No contingency planning
- Unsecured wireless networks

**Breach Notification Violations:**
- Delayed notification (beyond 60 days)
- Failure to notify affected individuals
- Inadequate breach documentation
- Failure to report to HHS

## Your Role in HIPAA Compliance

### Daily Responsibilities

**Protect PHI:**
- Only access information needed for your job
- Keep passwords confidential and change regularly
- Lock your workstation when away
- Never share login credentials
- Encrypt data on portable devices
- Secure paper records in locked drawers/cabinets

**Limit Disclosures:**
- Never discuss patient information in public areas
- Follow minimum necessary principle
- Verify identity before releasing information
- Document all disclosures
- Properly dispose of documents containing PHI

**Report Incidents:**
- Immediately report unauthorized access or suspected breaches
- Report lost or stolen devices containing patient information
- Report security vulnerabilities
- Report suspicious access patterns
- No retaliation for good faith reporting

### Training and Education

Organizations must provide HIPAA training that covers:
- Your organization's privacy and security policies
- How to identify and report breaches
- Your role in compliance
- Consequences of violations
- How to access confidential records properly

**Refresher training required:** At minimum, annually or when policies change

### Security Best Practices

**Passwords:**
- Use strong, unique passwords (minimum 12 characters)
- Include uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and special characters
- Change every 60-90 days
- Never reuse previous passwords
- Never write down passwords

**Device Security:**
- Use PIN or biometric authentication
- Lock devices automatically after 10 minutes of inactivity
- Enable full-disk encryption
- Keep software and security patches updated
- Use VPN for remote access
- Don't leave devices unattended in public

**Email Security:**
- Don't send unencrypted ePHI via email
- Use secure messaging systems when available
- Verify recipient email address before sending
- Use clear recipient fields carefully (avoid reply-all)
- Be cautious of phishing attempts
- Verify sender identity for unexpected requests

**Physical Security:**
- Keep office doors locked when handling PHI
- Use privacy screens on monitors
- Don't leave records on desks unattended
- Shred documents containing PHI
- Use secure fax machines with confirmation
- Control access to printer and fax areas

**Mobile Devices:**
- Never store unencrypted PHI
- Use remote wipe capabilities
- Enable automatic screen lock
- Keep OS and apps updated
- Use organization-approved apps only
- Report lost or stolen devices immediately

### Breach Response

If you discover a breach:

1. **Immediately stop** unauthorized access
2. **Preserve evidence** - Don't disturb the scene
3. **Document details**:
   - What happened
   - When it was discovered
   - Who was involved
   - What information was affected
   - Potential impact
4. **Report to management** or designated security officer
5. **Follow your organization's breach protocol**
6. **Cooperate with investigation**
7. **Don't communicate publicly** about the breach

## Key Takeaways

**Remember:**
- HIPAA protects patient privacy and security of health information
- You must minimize access to only necessary information
- Patient rights include access to their records and notification of breaches
- Violations carry significant financial and criminal penalties
- Your role is critical in maintaining compliance
- Training and awareness are ongoing requirements
- When in doubt, ask your compliance officer

**Questions?** Contact your organization's HIPAA Privacy Officer or Compliance Department.
